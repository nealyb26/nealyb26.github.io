Ancient Mortars

Classification of Ancient Building Materials
View Code

Overview

Ancient Mortars was a deep learning project that used image classification to study mortar composition from archaeological samples collected at Caesarea Maritima. The work focused on identifying fine-grained particle types from a large dataset of microscope-style particle images generated through dynamic image analysis.

The sample set came from a mix of historically distinct contexts at the site, including the Crusader cathedral, the Crusader fortress, the temple platform, and the Herodian wall on the northern side of the platform. Those samples captured mortar traditions associated with Roman, Jewish, Byzantine, Abbasid-Fatimid Muslim, and Crusader construction at the same ancient port city.

Approach

The dataset contained hundreds of thousands of images per particle type, so the main challenge was not simply model selection but building a pipeline that could learn from noisy, repeated observations of the same particle as it moved through the imaging system. Previously, the team had evaluated the effectiveness of both convolutional models and transformer-based architectures; however, I was tasked with investigating the effectiveness of fine-tuning google/vit-base-patch16-224-in21k on the four high-level mortar classes.

That modeling choice reflected the structure of the problem: the classes were visually subtle, the image volume was large, and the useful signal lived in texture, shape, and local variation rather than in obvious object boundaries. The result was a classifier that could separate kurkar, sand, olivepress residue, and volcanic ash from the image stream with strong accuracy.

Results

  • Fine-tuned google/vit-base-patch16-224-in21k on a four-class mortar classification task
  • Worked with a large-scale image dataset derived from dynamic particle analysis
  • Converted a materials-science style image stream into archaeologically meaningful categories
  • Achieved 92% accuracy on the high-level classification task
  • Created a model workflow that could be extended to other ancient material studies
